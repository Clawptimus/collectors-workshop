Commodore 64 Computers

Repair, keyboard service, and collector guide for the legendary home computer

The Best-Selling Computer of All Time

The Commodore 64 sold somewhere between 12 and 17 million units during its production run from 1982 to 1994. No other single computer model has come close to these numbers. For a generation, the C64 was computing — it was the machine that introduced millions to programming, gaming, and digital creativity. For a deeper dive, Commodore 64 Computers covers teardown, common failures, and modern upgrades in detail.

Today, the Commodore 64 enjoys a vibrant retro computing scene. Original hardware is still plentiful and affordable, making it an excellent entry point for vintage computer collecting. And unlike many vintage computers, the C64 has a vast library of software that's still accessible and enjoyable today.

C64 Variations

The "Breadbin": The original 1982 C64 with its distinctive beige case and brown keyboard. Early models have some differences in the PCB layout and are prized by purists.

The C64C: The 1986 restyling with a sleeker white case and lighter keyboard. Functionally identical but visually distinct — collectors often want both.

The SX-64: The portable version, complete with built-in monitor and disk drive. Heavy, rare, and highly collectible.

The C64 Games System: A console version that plugged directly into a TV. Commercially unsuccessful and relatively rare today.

Common Repairs

Power supply: The original C64 power supplies are notorious for failing and taking the computer with them. Use a modern replacement or have your original rebuilt with modern regulation.

PLA chip: The 906114-01 PLA is the most common failure point. Symptoms include a black screen or garbage on startup. Modern replacements are available and recommended.

Capacitors: After 40 years, electrolytic capacitors are reaching end of life. A full recap is good preventive maintenance for any C64 you plan to use regularly.

Keyboard: The original Mitsumi keyboards can develop bad contacts. Cleaning with contact cleaner often helps, but some keys may need their contacts repaired or replaced.

Building a C64 Collection

Start with a working C64C — they're plentiful, affordable, and reliable. Add a 1541 disk drive (or a modern SD-based replacement like the SD2IEC). The 1702 monitor is the classic pairing, but any composite monitor or modern HDMI solution works.

Software is part of the experience. Original disks are collectible, but modern solutions like the Ultimate 64 or SD2IEC let you access the vast C64 software library easily.

Consider the peripherals: the 1351 mouse, various joysticks, printers, and modems all expand the experience. The Commodore 64 was a system, not just a computer, and collecting the ecosystem is part of the fun.
