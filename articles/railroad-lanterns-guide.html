Railroad Lanterns

Dietz and Adlake identification guide — history, markings, variations, and collector values

Icons of American Railroading

Railroad lanterns are functional art — rugged tools built to withstand the harshest conditions while providing reliable light for the men who kept the trains running. Today, they're prized by collectors for their historical significance, their variety, and their undeniable visual appeal. For collectors looking to go deeper, Railroad Lanterns covers teardown, common failures, and modern upgrades in detail.

The two dominant names in American railroad lanterns are Dietz and Adlake. Between them, they produced millions of lanterns that illuminated railroads across North America from the 1870s through the mid-20th century.

Dietz Lanterns

R.H. Dietz Company of Syracuse, New York, was the largest lantern manufacturer in the United States. Their "Traffic Gard" and "Vesta" models are the most commonly encountered railroad lanterns today.

Key models to know:

  • Dietz Vesta: The classic cold blast lantern, produced from 1897 into the 1950s. Look for variations in the top lift lever, the globe guard design, and the base markings.
  • Dietz Traffic Gard: The standard railroad lantern from the 1920s-50s. Available in multiple colors and with various railroad markings.
  • Dietz Little Wizard: A smaller lantern often used by brakemen. Highly collectible in railroad-marked versions.

Dietz lanterns are dated by their patent markings, the style of the lift lever, and the design of the globe guard. Early examples have brass construction; later ones are steel.

Adlake Lanterns

Adams & Westlake (Adlake) of Chicago was the other major lantern manufacturer. Their "Adlake Reliable" and "Adlake Kero" are classics of railroad lighting.

Key models to know:

  • Adlake Reliable: The standard railroad lantern from the 1920s-60s. Look for variations in the frame design, the burner, and the base markings.
  • Adlake Kero: A kerosene-burning lantern with distinctive styling. Earlier versions are brass; later ones are steel.
  • Adlake Switch Lamp: Larger lamps used for signaling. More complex and visually impressive than hand lanterns.

Adlake lanterns are dated by their frame design, the style of the burner, and the markings on the base. The company changed their logo style over the years, which helps with dating.

Railroad Markings

The most valuable railroad lanterns are those marked with specific railroad names. "SP Co" (Southern Pacific), "AT&SF" (Santa Fe), "CB&Q" (Burlington Route), "PRR" (Pennsylvania Railroad) — these markings transform a common lantern into a desirable collectible.

Beware of fakes. Unscrupulous sellers have been known to add railroad markings to plain lanterns. Authentic markings are cast into the metal, not painted on. Study known authentic examples to develop an eye for genuine markings.

Condition and Value

Original condition is key. Original paint, original globes, and original burners all add value. However, lanterns were working tools, and honest wear is acceptable — in fact, a lantern with authentic patina often displays better than an over-restored example.

Common Dietz and Adlake lanterns without railroad markings can be found for $20-50. Railroad-marked examples start around $50-100 and can reach hundreds for rare roads or unusual colors. Switch lamps and specialized signaling devices command premiums.
