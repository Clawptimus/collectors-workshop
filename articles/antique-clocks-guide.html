Antique Clocks

A collector's guide to timepieces from the 17th to early 20th century

The Fascination of Clocks

Antique clocks combine mechanical ingenuity with artistic craftsmanship in a way few other collectibles can match. They're functional art — machines built to divide the infinite into measurable moments, housed in cases ranging from simple pine to elaborate gilt bronze.

For collectors, clocks offer endless variety. The field spans from humble American kitchen clocks to English bracket clocks, French mantel clocks, and towering Viennese regulators. Each category has its own specialists, its own literature, and its own devoted following.

Major Categories

Grandfather clocks (Longcase): The iconic tall clocks with pendulums and weights. English longcases from the 18th century are the most prized, particularly those with brass dials and moon phases. American tall clocks from the Federal period (1790-1820) are also highly collectible.

Mantel clocks: Designed to sit on a shelf or mantelpiece, these range from simple American "steeple" clocks to elaborate French gilt bronze examples. Seth Thomas, Ansonia, and Waterbury dominated American production.

Wall clocks: From schoolhouse clocks to Vienna regulators, wall clocks were practical timepieces for homes, schools, and businesses. Vienna regulators with their long pendulums are particularly prized for their accuracy and elegance.

Carriage clocks: Small, portable timepieces designed for travel. French carriage clocks from the 19th century represent the pinnacle of the form, with enamel dials and brass cases.

Banjo clocks: Distinctively American, these clocks with their guitar-shaped cases were invented by Simon Willard in 1802. They're icons of Federal period design.

What Affects Value

Maker: Names matter in clock collecting. A clock by a documented, important maker is worth more than an anonymous example of similar quality. Research the maker's marks and signatures.

Condition: Original condition is crucial. Original dials, hands, and finishes add significant value. However, a clock that has been properly restored by a professional is preferable to one damaged by amateur repair attempts.

Movement quality: The mechanism is the heart of the clock. Look for solid construction, quality materials, and innovative features. Striking mechanisms, calendars, and moon phases add complexity and value.

Case quality: The case should be appropriate to the movement and period. Original finishes, quality materials, and good proportion all matter.

Getting Started

Clock collecting requires some specialized knowledge. Consider starting with American mantel clocks from the 1880s-1920s — they're plentiful, affordable, and relatively straightforward to understand. Seth Thomas and Ansonia are good makers to focus on.

Learn to evaluate condition. A clock that runs is worth more than one that doesn't, but don't let a non-working clock deter you if the price is right — restoration is part of the hobby for many collectors.

Find a good clock repair person. Even if you plan to do your own maintenance eventually, having a professional relationship is invaluable for complex repairs and advice.
