Vintage Typewriter Collecting

A comprehensive guide to identifying, valuing, and collecting mechanical writing machines

Why Collect Typewriters?

Vintage typewriters represent a perfect storm of collectibility: they're mechanical marvels, they're historically significant, they're aesthetically striking, and they're still functional today. Unlike many collectibles that sit on shelves, a typewriter can still perform the task it was built for nearly a century ago.

The typewriter revolutionized communication, business, and literature. Every machine tells a story about the era it was built in, from the ornate cast iron of the 1890s to the sleek mid-century modern designs of the 1950s and 60s.

Major Manufacturers to Know

Remington: The first commercial typewriter manufacturer, Remington produced machines from 1874 well into the 20th century. Early Remingtons are highly collectible, particularly the Remington No. 1 and No. 2.

Underwood: Dominant in the early 20th century, Underwood set the standard for modern typewriter design. Their machines are robust, plentiful, and excellent for beginners.

Royal: Known for innovation and quality, Royal typewriters from the 1920s-1950s are particularly prized. The Royal Quiet De Luxe is considered one of the finest portable typewriters ever made.

Smith-Corona: The leading portable typewriter manufacturer, Smith-Corona produced millions of machines. Their 1950s-60s models are excellent entry points for new collectors.

Olympia: German precision engineering at its finest. Olympia typewriters, particularly the SM series, are favorites among writers and collectors alike.

What Makes a Typewriter Valuable?

Rarity: Limited production models, early machines, and typewriters from defunct manufacturers command premiums. The first generation of any major design change often becomes collectible.

Condition: Original condition is king. A machine with original paint, decals, and minimal wear is worth significantly more than a restored example. Working condition matters too — a typewriter that types is always worth more than a parts machine.

Provenance: Typewriters owned by famous writers, used in significant historical contexts, or with documented histories can command extraordinary prices.

Aesthetics: Color, design, and visual appeal matter. Machines in unusual colors (pink, turquoise, red) or with striking industrial design often sell for more than their utilitarian counterparts.

Getting Started

Start with what appeals to you. If you love the steampunk aesthetic of early machines, focus on 1890s-1910s index typewriters and early standards. If you prefer mid-century modern design, collect 1950s-60s portables. If you want to use your collection, focus on reliable machines from the 1940s-1960s.

Learn to assess condition. Check for rust, pitting, missing parts, and frozen mechanisms. A typewriter with a frozen carriage or seized typebars can often be freed with patience and proper lubrication, but severe rust or missing parts may be dealbreakers.

Buy the best example you can afford. It's better to have one excellent machine than five project machines that never get restored.
